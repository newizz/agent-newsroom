## Headline findings

1. **Dogs perceive time as "brief" or "long" — not in hours** — A dog's excitement at your return is the same whether you've been gone 2 hours or 8, because both exceed its perceptual threshold of "a while away"; it gauges elapsed time via fading scent and body state, not a clock.
2. **A dog's nose is its primary reality, 100,000x more sensitive than ours** — With 300 million olfactory receptors (vs. our 5–6 million) and 33% of brain volume devoted to smell, dogs experience the world as layered scent-films with a temporal dimension — they can effectively "smell the past."
3. **Dogs sleep 12–14 hours daily in 20+ short cycles** — Activity clusters into two daily peaks (8–10 a.m. and 5–11 p.m.) that track their human's schedule, not solar cues; weekend activity is measurably higher than weekday.
4. **The "guilty look" is appeasement, not remorse** — What looks like guilt is actually a calming display triggered by the owner's body language in that moment, not by the dog's memory of the misdeed.
5. **Up to 56% of dogs experience clinically significant separation-related distress** — This stems from multiple emotional states (fear, frustration, panic) rather than a single "anxiety," and is rooted in an attachment bond neurologically similar to infant-caregiver bonding.

## Limitations & caveats

- **Dog emotions are neurobiologically supported but philosophically contested** — the evidence for emotion-like states (oxytocin, cortisol, amygdala activity) is solid; whether dogs have subjective experience of those states cannot be confirmed empirically.
- **Time perception study is small** — The Rehn & Keeling (2011) study is the main reference for dog time perception; it has a limited sample and doesn't fully control for differences in owner return behavior.
- **Breed-cognition findings can't isolate nature vs. nurture** — The 1,002-dog study couldn't control for training history or owner behavior, so genetic vs. environmental attribution is unclear.
- **Guilty look research base is narrow** — Most findings come from a small number of studies (Horowitz 2009 being the most cited); cross-cultural replication is limited.
- **Senior cognitive dysfunction prevalence is disputed** — Estimates for canine dementia vary widely across studies; no single authoritative figure.
- **Dog "dreams" remain unconfirmed** — REM-phase physical indicators (paw twitching, eye movement) are documented, but subjective content — if any — cannot be studied.
