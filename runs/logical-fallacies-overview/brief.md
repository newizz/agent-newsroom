## Original prompt
> ภาพรวมเรื่องตรรกะวิบัติ ความหมายของตรรกะวิบัติแต่ละประเภท และตัวอย่างในชีวิตจริง

## Refined question
What are logical fallacies, what are the most important types along with their definitions and distinguishing features, and how do they manifest in real-life situations such as political debates, advertising, social media, and everyday arguments?

## Scope
- **In scope:**
  - Definition of "logical fallacy" and why it undermines arguments
  - Major categorical split: formal fallacies (invalid logical form) vs. informal fallacies (flawed reasoning content)
  - The ~15–20 most common and practically important fallacy types, each with: Thai name, English name, concise definition, and 1–2 real-life examples
  - Real-life domains: politics/policy debates, advertising/marketing claims, social media discourse, everyday interpersonal arguments
  - Brief practical guide: how to spot and respond to a logical fallacy in conversation
- **Out of scope:**
  - Formal symbolic logic or mathematical proof theory
  - Exhaustive academic taxonomy of 100+ named fallacies
  - Deep philosophical disputes about whether a given pattern is "always" a fallacy
  - Fallacies in formal deductive proofs (mathematics, computer science)

## Audience
General Thai-speaking readers — students, curious adults, and professionals who want stronger critical-thinking skills. Assumed to have no prior philosophy background; they care about practical recognition and real examples more than academic terminology. The dashboard should be accessible to a high-school student yet still useful to a university-level reader.

## Success criteria
A successful dashboard will:
- Let the reader quickly understand what a logical fallacy is and why it matters within 30 seconds of landing on the page
- Present each fallacy type with a clear name (Thai + English), a one-sentence definition, and at least one vivid, relatable real-world example — scannable as a reference card
- Include an interactive or organized way to browse by fallacy category (formal vs. informal, or by real-world domain) so readers can explore at their own pace
- Leave the reader able to identify at least 3–5 common fallacies the next time they encounter them in the wild

## Key questions Research must answer
1. What is the standard definition of a logical fallacy, and what is the difference between a formal and an informal fallacy?
2. Which 15–20 fallacy types are most commonly encountered and most practically important for general audiences? (e.g., ad hominem, straw man, appeal to authority, false dichotomy, slippery slope, appeal to emotion, hasty generalization, circular reasoning, red herring, post hoc ergo propter hoc, appeal to nature, bandwagon, tu quoque, loaded question, false analogy)
3. For each selected fallacy: what is the precise definition, what logical error does it commit, and what distinguishes it from superficially similar fallacies?
4. For each selected fallacy: what are 1–2 concrete, culturally relatable real-life examples — ideally mixing global (politics, advertising, social media) with everyday Thai-context scenarios?
5. Are there particular domains (Thai politics, consumer advertising, online debates) where certain fallacies appear with especially high frequency?
6. What practical strategies help a reader recognize and calmly rebut a logical fallacy mid-conversation without derailing the discussion?

## Assumptions made
- Assumed general/mixed audience (not academic philosophers) because the prompt emphasized "real-life examples" over theory.
- Assumed Thai language is the primary presentation language for the dashboard, with English fallacy names shown as secondary labels.
- Assumed coverage of ~15–20 major fallacy types provides sufficient depth for "deep" mode without becoming an exhaustive catalogue.

## Open risks
- Some fallacy names lack widely agreed Thai translations; Research should note multiple translation variants where they exist.
- Real-life examples touching on Thai politics may need careful framing to remain illustrative rather than partisan.
- "Deep" mode may surface dozens of fallacy subtypes; Research should flag which ones to merge or omit to keep the dashboard focused.
