## Headline findings

1. **Ethiopia grew it, Yemen brewed it** — The Coffea arabica plant evolved in the Ethiopian highlands, but the earliest credible written record of coffee as a beverage dates to 15th-century Yemeni Sufi monks, not to the popular Kaldi goat-herder legend (first written down in 1671 CE).
2. **Coffeehouses built modern capitalism** — Lloyd's of London (global insurance) and the London Stock Exchange both trace their institutional origins directly to specific London coffeehouses of the 1680s; by 1700 London had an estimated 2,000–3,000 coffeehouses.
3. **Brazil's empire started with a bouquet** — The entire Brazilian coffee industry — today 31% of global supply — traces to a 1727 diplomatic seduction in which a Portuguese officer allegedly obtained smuggled coffee seedlings hidden in a bouquet of flowers.
4. **Caffeine evolved at least five times independently** — A 2016 PNAS study found caffeine independently evolved in coffee, tea, cacao, guaraná, and citrus plants via entirely different biosynthetic pathways — one of nature's most celebrated examples of convergent evolution.
5. **The world's top coffee drinkers are Scandinavian, not American** — Despite the global dominance of US coffee brands, Americans rank only 22nd in per-capita consumption; Luxembourg leads at 12.5 kg/person/year and Finland's average resident drinks ~4.1 cups daily.

## Limitations & caveats

- The **Kaldi legend** (9th–10th century) is unverified folklore; no archaeological or textual evidence confirms Ethiopian coffee use before the 15th century. The dashboard presents it clearly as legend.
- The **"2.25 billion cups per day"** figure is a widely cited industry estimate attributed to the Specialty Coffee Association; the underlying methodology is not published in audited detail.
- **Arabica market share figures are in flux.** Historical averages put Arabica at ~70% of global production, but 2024/25 Tridge data shows a ~57/43 Arabica/Robusta split, reflecting a long-term trend toward Robusta driven partly by climate pressure. Both figures are cited with dates in the dashboard.
- **Thai and Southeast Asian coffee culture** (yen ta fo, oliang, condensed-milk iced coffee traditions) is under-represented, as English-language sources dominate the research corpus. A Thai-language research pass would enrich this dimension.
- The **colonial labor history** of coffee (enslaved African labor in Brazil and the Caribbean) is acknowledged in the research but is presented only briefly in the dashboard to preserve its general-audience tone; deeper treatment would be warranted for an academic or advocacy audience.
- Market value figures ($461 billion global market) come from a single commercial research report (Renub Research) and should be treated as an industry estimate.
