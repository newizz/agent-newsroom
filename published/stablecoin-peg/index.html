Three peg models

Fiat-backed (USDT, USDC) — every coin is supposedly redeemable for $1 in a bank. Peg is held by trust + arbitrage.

Crypto-backed (DAI) — over-collateralised with volatile crypto. If collateral falls in value, positions are liquidated automatically.

Algorithmic (UST, prior to collapse) — no real collateral; rely on supply elasticity via a paired token. Historically fragile.

Why arbitrage holds the peg

If USDC trades at $0.99 on the market, an authorised participant can buy 1M USDC for $990,000, redeem them at Circle for $1,000,000 in USD, and pocket $10,000 risk-free.

That demand pushes the market price back up to $1. Arbitrage works in both directions and is the single most important reason fiat-backed coins stay close to peg in normal markets.

Analysis

Stablecoins don't really maintain $1 directly — they maintain a credible promise of redemption. The actual price comes from arbitrage between the on-chain market and the off-chain (or smart-contract) redemption mechanism.

This means peg stability is mostly about three things: quality of reserves, speed of redemption, and market confidence that the first two hold. When any of these breaks, the peg breaks — sometimes spectacularly, as UST demonstrated by losing all $40B of its market cap in days.

The post-2022 trend has been toward more transparent, faster-attesting reserves (monthly → daily → real-time) and away from purely algorithmic designs.
