The Story of Coffee

From the highland forests of Ethiopia and the prayer halls of 15th-century Yemen, to the coffeehouses that incubated the Enlightenment and the 2.25 billion cups consumed every single day — coffee is far more than a drink. It is one of history's great engines of human connection, commerce, and culture.

Origins: Ethiopia & Yemen

The coffee plant Coffea arabica is indigenous to the highland forests of Ethiopia, growing wild at elevations between 1,000 and 2,000 metres in the southwestern highlands. The Kaffa and Oromo peoples likely consumed coffee cherries as a food paste long before any beverage was made from them. Yet the plant that grew in Africa would only become the world-altering drink we know today in a different country entirely.

The earliest credible written record of coffee as a brewed beverage comes from Yemeni Sufi texts of the mid-15th century.[^2] Monks of the Shadhiliyya order discovered that roasting the bean and brewing it into a hot drink sustained their long nights of devotional prayer and meditation. From Yemen's port cities, the practice spread northward to Mecca by 1414, where it entered Islamic pilgrimage networks and began its journey across the known world.[^3]

The beloved Kaldi legend — the Ethiopian goat-herder who observed his flock dancing after eating red berries from a wild bush — is the most popular origin story in coffee culture. It is also, strictly speaking, folklore. The story was first committed to writing in 1671 CE by a Maronite monk named Antoine Faustus Nairon, centuries after the events it claims to describe.[^1] Coffee historians treat it as explanatory myth rather than documented history. The distinction matters: Ethiopia is where the plant evolved; Yemen is where the beverage was born.

A 2020 genetic study (Scalabrin et al., Scientific Reports) confirmed Yemen's central role further: the vast majority of modern Coffea arabica varieties trace their lineage to early Yemeni cultivated strains, meaning that the genetic foundation of the world's coffee supply was largely shaped by those 15th-century Sufi gardens.[^3]

The Coffeehouse Revolution

No institution in history has punched quite so far above its weight as the coffeehouse. In the Ottoman world (kahvehane) and then in Europe, the coffeehouse created something radically new: a sober, egalitarian public space where men of different social classes could sit, talk, read, debate, and do business — for the price of a cup.

The first Ottoman coffeehouses opened in Constantinople (Istanbul) in 1554–55, established by two traders from Aleppo and Damascus during the reign of Suleiman the Magnificent.[^13] These establishments admitted merchants, scholars, students, and soldiers alike. They hosted chess, poetry recitals, political debate, and news exchange. So threatening did they appear to authority that Sultan Murad IV (r. 1623–40) made coffee consumption a capital offense — a ban that was largely unenforceable and ultimately failed.[^5]

In London, the first English coffeehouse opened in 1652, run by Pasqua Rosée in St Michael's Alley. By 1700, London alone had an estimated 2,000–3,000 coffeehouses. They were nicknamed “Penny Universities” because for the price of a penny admission and a cup, any man could join intellectual discourse of the highest order.[^5]

The institutional legacy is staggering: Lloyd's of London (global insurance market) began at Edward Lloyd's coffeehouse in 1686, where ship merchants exchanged maritime intelligence and arranged cargo insurance.[^4] The London Stock Exchange traces its origin to Jonathan's Coffee House around 1680, where stock prices were first publicly posted.[^4] Café Procope in Paris, opened 1686, became the salon where Voltaire, Rousseau, and Diderot refined Enlightenment philosophy. Isaac Newton was a regular at the Grecian Coffee House. Reuters news agency and Christie's auction house also trace their roots to specific London coffeehouses.[^5]

And then there was the English king who tried to shut it all down. In December 1675, King Charles II issued a royal proclamation suppressing coffeehouses, citing their role in spreading “false news and libelous matter.” The public outcry was so immediate and fierce that he was forced to retract the proclamation within 11 days, before it could even take effect.[^5]

Arabica vs. Robusta

Arabica Coffea arabica
  • Caffeine: ~1.5% by dry weight — lower, smoother buzz
  • Flavour: Complex, nuanced — fruity, floral, acidic notes
  • Altitude: 610–1,830m — high-altitude specialist
  • Market share: ~57–70% of global production
  • Origin: Ethiopian highlands; dominant in specialty coffee
  • Sensitivity: Vulnerable to climate change; needs narrow temperature band
[^16]
Robusta Coffea canephora
  • Caffeine: ~2.7% — nearly double Arabica; stronger kick
  • Flavour: Earthy, strong, bitter — less complexity, more body
  • Altitude: Sea level to 800m — highly adaptable
  • Market share: ~30–43% — rising as climate shifts
  • Main producers: Vietnam, Indonesia, West Africa
  • Uses: Espresso blends (thick crema), instant coffee
[^16]

Note on market share: Historical figures quote Arabica at ~70% of global production. Current 2024/25 production data (Arabica: 99.9M bags vs. Robusta: 76.4M bags) implies a ~57/43 split — indicating Robusta's share has grown substantially, partly driven by climate change forcing Arabica cultivation to higher, narrower altitudes. The trend toward parity is real and accelerating.[^6][^16]
